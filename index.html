The Evolution of Stars

Stars form from collapsing clouds of gas and dust known as nebulae. Once nuclear fusion begins in their cores, they enter the main sequence phase, where they spend most of their lifetimes converting hydrogen into helium. As they evolve, their structure and appearance change dramatically depending on mass.

Low and intermediate-mass stars expand into red giants before shedding their outer layers and ending their lives as white dwarfs. In contrast, high-mass stars evolve much more rapidly and may end in powerful supernova explosions, leaving behind neutron stars or black holes.
