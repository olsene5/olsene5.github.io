Star Type O

O-type stars are the hottest and most massive stars in the universe. Their surface temperatures can exceed 30,000 K, giving them a bright blue color. These stars burn their nuclear fuel very quickly and live for only a few million years. 10 Lacertae is one of these bright O type stars. The Great Lacerta Nebula with 10 10 Lacertae at its center glows red, becasue the Lacertae's bright light excites the surrounding hydrogen gas.

Star Type B

B-type stars are very hot and luminous stars with surface temperatures between about 10,000 and 30,000 K. They appear blue or blue-white in color and are typically several times more massive than the Sun. Rigel is a B type star in the constellation orion nearing the end of its life. it has expanded into a supergiant and fluctuates in brightness.

Star Type A

A-type stars have surface temperatures between roughly 7,500 and 10,000 K. They appear white or bluish-white and are often among the brightest stars visible from Earth. Sirius, an A type star, is the brightest star in the night sky. It is a binary star, and orbits its companion Sirius B.

Star Type F

F-type stars are slightly hotter than the Sun, with surface temperatures between about 6,000 and 7,500 K. They appear yellow-white and are often found on the main sequence of the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram. Polaris, or the north star, is a F type supergiant star located near the north celestial pole.

The Sun

Star Type G

The Sun is a G-type main sequence star located at the center of our solar system. With a surface temperature of about 5,800 K, it appears yellow when viewed from space.

Star Type K

K-type stars are cooler than the Sun, with surface temperatures between about 3,900 and 5,200 K. They appear orange in color and are slightly smaller and less massive than G-type stars. Arcturus is a K type star and the 4th brightest star in the sky. it can be seen by following the curve of the Big Dipper's handle, and appears orange to yellow in color.

Star Type M

M-type stars are the coolest and most common stars in the universe. Their surface temperatures are below about 3,900 K, giving them a reddish color. Barnard's star is a M type star and the second nearest star to the sun. Like the sun, it also has planets orbiting it.

Distribution of star Types.

Not all star types are equally common in the universe. Most stars are small, cool red dwarfs belonging to spectral class M. These stars make up the majority of stars in the Milky Way galaxy.
