Summary of the HR Diagram

The Hertzsprung-Russell diagram is a foundational tool in astronomy that reveals how stars change over the course of their lifetimes. Although stars may begin their lives in different regions of the diagram depending on their mass, they do not remain fixed in one place. Instead, they move through distinct evolutionary paths as their internal structure changes. These movements reflect major transitions such as the exhaustion of hydrogen fuel, core contraction, and the expansion of outer layers. Despite the wide variety of stellar types, the HR diagram shows that their evolution follows predictable patterns. Low-mass stars evolve slowly along, while high-mass stars change rapidly and die more violently.

Explore the lifes of stars with an HR diagram.

Y-axis

The vertical axis of the HR diagram represents a star's luminosity, or total energy output. Luminosity is often measured relative to the Sun, with stars above the Sun being more luminous and stars below being less luminous.

X-axis

The horizontal axis of the HR diagram represents the surface temperature of stars. Temperatures decrease from left to right, and so does their color which goes from a hot blue to cool red.

Tracks

Stars do not remain fixed on the HR diagram. As they evolve, their temperature and luminosity change, causing them to move across the diagram over time.

Branches Section

Above the main sequence are the giant and supergiant branches, which contain stars that have expanded and become much larger late in their lives. In the lower-left corner of the diagram are white dwarfs, small but extremely hot stellar remnants.
